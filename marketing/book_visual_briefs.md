# VISUAL CONCEPTS — CREATIVE BRIEFS FOR "YOU ARE WHAT YOU EAT"

*10 visual concepts with full specifications for a graphic designer or illustrator*
*Each brief includes: purpose, placement, layout, colour, typography, and content*

---

## VISUAL STYLE GUIDE (applies to all visuals)

**Colour Palette:**
- Background: Charcoal (#1A1A2E) or Off-white (#F5F0EB) depending on print choice
- Green (Group 1 / positive): #2ECC71
- Amber (Group 2 / neutral): #F39C12
- Orange (Group 3 / caution): #E67E22
- Red (Group 4 / warning): #E74C3C
- Deep Red (critical): #C0392B
- Accent Gold: #D4A843
- Text: White on dark, Charcoal on light

**Typography:**
- Headlines: Bold, condensed sans-serif (e.g., Oswald, Bebas Neue, or Knockout)
- Body: Clean sans-serif (e.g., Proxima Nova, Montserrat, or Source Sans Pro)
- Callouts: Mono or semi-bold italic

**Overall tone:** Serious, authoritative, slightly cinematic. Think documentary infographic, not children's health leaflet. No clip art. No cartoons. No emojis.

## VISUAL 1 — THE REFINERY COMPARISON

**Purpose:** The book's most powerful visual argument. Shows that vegetable oil refining and fuel refining follow nearly identical industrial processes.

**Placement:** Chapter 3 (The Refinery), full page or double-page spread

**Layout:** Two parallel vertical flowcharts, side by side.

**Left column: "VEGETABLE OIL REFINING"**
| Step | Label |
|------|-------|
| 1 | Crush seeds into fine meal |
| 2 | Wash in hexane (petroleum solvent) |
| 3 | Evaporate solvent |
| 4 | Degum with acid/steam |
| 5 | Neutralise with caustic soda |
| 6 | Bleach with activated clays |
| 7 | Deodorise at 200-260C |
| Result | Bottled as "vegetable oil" |

**Right column: "FUEL REFINING"**
| Step | Label |
|------|-------|
| 1 | Distil crude oil |
| 2 | Use solvents/catalysts to separate |
| 3 | Remove impurities |
| 4 | Treat with chemicals |
| 5 | Neutralise acids |
| 6 | Bleach/filter to remove colour |
| 7 | Heat to high temperatures |
| Result | Stored as fuel |

**Design notes:**
- Use identical visual treatment for both columns (same box shapes, same arrow styles, same colours)
- The point is the *sameness* — the reader should struggle to tell them apart at a glance
- Subtle amber/orange glow on the left, industrial grey on the right
- At the bottom, a single line: *"One is sold as food. The other is sold as fuel."*

## VISUAL 2 — THE NOVA CLASSIFICATION SCALE

**Purpose:** The definitive reference visual for the NOVA system. Should be clear enough to understand in 5 seconds.

**Placement:** Chapter 2 (The Scale They Don't Want You to Understand), full page

**Layout:** Four horizontal bands/tiers, stacked vertically, widening from top to bottom (or intensifying in colour)

**Content:**

| Group | Colour | Label | Examples (with small icons) |
|-------|--------|-------|---------------------------|
| 1 | Green | UNPROCESSED / MINIMALLY PROCESSED | Apple, Egg, Fish, Rice, Milk |
| 2 | Amber | PROCESSED CULINARY INGREDIENTS | Olive oil, Butter, Salt, Sugar, Flour |
| 3 | Orange | PROCESSED FOODS | Cheese, Tinned fish, Fresh bread, Jam |
| 4 | Deep Red | ULTRA-PROCESSED FOOD PRODUCTS | Fizzy drinks, Crisps, Chicken nuggets, Protein bars, Cereal |

**Design notes:**
- Group 4 band should be visually louder/heavier than the others — thicker, bolder, more alarming
- A dotted line between Group 3 and Group 4 labeled: *"THE LINE THAT MATTERS"*
- Small text at bottom: *"Source: NOVA Classification System (Monteiro et al., University of Sao Paulo)"*
- The key visual message: there's a SHARP divide between 3 and 4, not a gradual slide

## VISUAL 5 — THE TIMELINE (UPF Rise vs. Disease Rise)

**Purpose:** Shows the parallel rise of UPF consumption and chronic disease rates over the last 50-70 years.

**Placement:** Chapter 1 or Chapter 6, full page or double-page spread

**Layout:** Dual-axis line graph on a dark background.

**X-axis:** Decades (1960s — 2020s)

**Line 1 (Amber):** "UPF Market Share / % of Household Calories" — rising sharply from ~10% to ~55%+

**Line 2 (Red):** "Obesity / Type 2 Diabetes / Metabolic Disease Rates" — rising in near-perfect parallel

**Key markers on the timeline:**
- 1960s: UPF consumption low, chronic disease rare
- 1980s: UPF expansion begins (deregulation, cheap corn syrup)
- 2000s: UPFs dominate supermarket shelves
- 2020s: 50%+ of calories from UPFs; chronic disease at record levels

**Design notes:**
- The two lines should track so closely that the visual argument is undeniable
- DO NOT make medical claims — label the Y-axis as "relative prevalence (indexed)" to show the pattern, not the proof
- Bottom note: *"Correlation is not causation. But when the curves are this close, it demands explanation."*
- Stylised/simplified — this is not a scientific paper, it's a visual argument

## VISUAL 7 — THE LABEL COMPARISON

**Purpose:** Side-by-side comparison of a real food label vs a UPF label, with annotations.

**Placement:** Chapter 9 or Chapter 13 (The Tools That Help), double-page spread

**Layout:** Two product labels side by side.

**Left: REAL BREAD**
```
Ingredients: Wheat flour, water, salt, yeast.
```
Annotation: *"4 ingredients. All recognisable. Group 3."*

**Right: SUPERMARKET "HEALTHY" BREAD**
```
Ingredients: Wheat flour, water, yeast, salt, soya flour, 
vegetable oil (rapeseed), emulsifiers (E472e, E481), 
flour treatment agent (ascorbic acid), preservative 
(calcium propionate), dextrose.
```
Annotation: *"12 ingredients. Emulsifiers. Industrial oil. Preservative. Dextrose. Group 4."*

**Design notes:**
- Make the labels look realistic — simulate actual back-of-pack labels
- The contrast should be immediate and visceral
- A third option could show: *"What to look for in 10 seconds"* with the red flags circled

## PRODUCTION NOTES FOR DESIGNER

1. **Print format:** These visuals are designed for standard trade paperback (6"x9" or similar). Ensure all text is readable at print size.
2. **Digital format:** All visuals should also be exported as standalone images (1080x1350px for Instagram, 1200x628px for Facebook/X) for social media promotion.
3. **Colour consistency:** All visuals should use the colour palette defined at the top of this document. NOVA colours (Green/Amber/Orange/Red) must be consistent between the NOVA scale visual, the app's UI, and all other book visuals.
4. **Accessibility:** Ensure sufficient contrast ratios. Add pattern fills to pie charts and graphs for colour-blind readers.
5. **Tear-out pages:** The checklist (Visual 6), calendar (Visual 8), and swaps table (Visual 10) should be designed for practical use — high contrast, minimal decoration, clear typography.
